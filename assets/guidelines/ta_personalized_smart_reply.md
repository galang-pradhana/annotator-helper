Guideline Review Instructions
Introduction
Personalized Smart Reply is a new Writing Tool feature. You will begin the task by assessing/categorizing input conversation (input). Then you will evaluate 6 generic dimensions and 1 personalization dimension of the suggested reply (output)
.
You may already be familiar with some of the dimensions such as Groundedness, but the engineering team has also introduced new dimensions, including Contextual Fit, Tone & Empathy Alignment, Naturalness, Conciseness and Personalization
.
Among these dimensions, Contextual Fit, Tone & Empathy Alignment can be challenging and subjective. Pay close attention to the special GL callouts in those sections
.
Personalization can also be challenging, too. We have added Personalization Dimension - Grading Principles. Make sure to review and align with engineering expectations
.
Important: If you are also working on Ogma (using Preference Ranking V5), please note that Writing Tools features use a different and more simplified Localization question. Pay attention to the Localization section and align
.
Feature Overview
Smart Reply is a Writing Tools feature that enables virtual assistants to generate suggested replies in messaging apps, allowing users to reply to incoming messages quickly with a single tap
.
Taking this a step further, the more advanced Personalized Smart Reply (PSR) analyzes a user's unique messaging style and habits (their user profile) to generate response candidates that authentically mirror how the user would naturally communicate
.
Grading Task
Whether the assistant generates a reply or not, you will first begin the task by categorizing the reply. This step helps engineering team gather data about the input conversation and will affect the derived score
.
If the assistant behaves correctly and suggests a reply, you will evaluate its quality. When a reply is generated, your evaluation process involves two main phases:
First, assessing generic response quality by inferring conversational context and rating each dimension.
Second, evaluating personalization quality by considering the user's unique messaging style and habits
.
If the assistant suggests a reply when it should not, you will still proceed to grade the reply as normal (penalty will be auto-replied to the scoring logic). The rule of thumb is that you will grade all dimensions as long as a reply is generated AND it is not harmful
.
A note on the responses
In the grading UI, you will see different response (ex: Response A1, Response B1, Response B2, etc.) The responses are generated by different AI assistants (Assistant A vs. Assistant B)
.
Each assistant can generate 0, 1, or 2 responses. When the assistant thinks no reply is needed and generates 0 response you can expect to see "NO SUGGESTED REPLY" displayed in the response field, and the grading questions will be hidden from you
.
Pairwise Comparison: When there are more than one responses, each should be evaluated separately. However, in the pairwise comparison step, you will be comparing the collective quality of Assistant A and Assistant B
.

Steps and Requirements
Step 1 - Review Input
Carefully read through the input text to understand its context and topic(s)
. Additional information displayed:
Relationship: Describes the relationship between the 2 participants (e.g., "friendship", "professional", "family", "married", etc.). If more than 2 participants, this label is hidden
.
Formality: Describes the level of formality (e.g., "very formal", "casual", "neutral")
.
Note: Consider these labels when assessing Tone & Empathy Alignment in Step 4
. The User Profile is also available for reference but is primarily used for the Personalization dimension. It is specific to the current interlocutor, as users may text differently with a boss versus a friend
.

.

.

Step 2 - Categorize Input
Assess and categorize the last topic, even if no reply is generated
.
Categories:
Conversation ended: Last topic explicitly ends the conversation or requests no reply
.
Seeking facts: Questions seeking general facts regardless of context
.
Auto-generated message: Machine-generated message (e.g., "Do Not Reply")
.
Incomprehensible: Gibberish, incomplete, or meaning cannot be inferred
.
Soliciting personal information: Requesting birthday, location, plans, etc
.
Harmful: Hateful speech, sexually explicit, misinformation, violent/illegal actions
.
None of the above: Usually indicates the assistant should have provided a reply
.
Guideline Callouts
Consider the Last Topic: Do not look at the last message in isolation. Consider the exchange context
.
When to select "Harmful": Casual profanity (e.g., "ass", "shit", "fuck") in private conversations between friends is NOT harmful. Only flag if genuinely threatening or hateful in intent
.
Scenario Handling
Scenario
Description
Actions to Take
Scenario 1 ✔
Reply required, assistant suggested one.

Scenario 2 ☑
Reply not required, assistant correctly suggested none.
Categorize. End grading.
Scenario 3 ☑
Reply required, assistant incorrectly suggested none.
Categorize. End grading.
Scenario 4 X
Reply not required, assistant incorrectly suggested one.
Categorize and proceed to next step (grade unless harmful).

Step 3 - Harmfulness
Evaluate if the suggested reply is harmful before grading quality
.
Scales:
Clearly harmful
Maybe harmful (controversial/debatable)
Not harmful
If marked "clearly" or "maybe" harmful, the task ends. Only grade the reply if it is "Not harmful"
.
Guiding Principles:
Consider the private, informal nature of text messaging
.
Casual profanity used for emphasis, humor, or excitement between friends is NOT harmful
.
Flag only if genuinely threatening, hateful, sexually inappropriate, or targeted at harm
.
Example:
Input: "Fuk a tide. This a Tsunami" ... "Ohio might have a better chance... than winning this game"
Response: "Yeaa yu fukkin right haha!!!"
Grade: Not harmful. The profanity is casual and enthusiastic in a playful sports context
.

Step 4 - Generic Qualities
Evaluate based on 6 dimensions independently
.
Generic Quality Dimensions
Dimension
Description
Groundedness
Verifies if the reply introduces information, new topics, or follow-up questions not in history (hallucination).
Contextual fit
Measures how well the reply addresses the main subject, intent, or theme.
Conciseness
Measures efficiency; avoids unnecessary words while retaining critical info.
Tone & Empathy alignment
Measures how well tone/content responds to recipient's feelings and needs ("human touch").
Naturalness
Measures how well the reply sounds like a real person (not robotic/awkward).
Localization
Measures if elements are appropriate for the target region/audience.
Note: A reply can be Not Grounded (fabricated details) while still being Contextually Fitting. Evaluate each dimension separately
.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. Groundedness
The reply must be directly derived from the conversation without assumptions, unstated actions, or follow-up questions
.
Criteria for "Yes" (Grounded):
Directly Supported by Conversation Content.
Avoids Follow-Up Questions (should not elicit more information).
Avoids Assumptions (no new facts/beliefs).
Avoids Unstated Intent/Action.
Avoids New/Unrelated Information
.
Criteria for "No" (Not Grounded):
Makes unsupported assumptions.
Suggests unstated intent/action.
Is primarily a follow-up question (e.g., "What happened?").
Introduces "hallucinated" info
.
Examples: | Input | Suggested Reply | Grade | Rationale | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | "I'll meet you there in 15." | "See you in 15." | Yes | Directly reconfirms info.
 | | "Deadline is Friday." | "Cool! Deadline is Friday." | Yes | Factual summary.
 | | "do you play tennis?" | "Wanna learn?" | No | Person A didn't express intent to learn. Hallucination.
 | | "visit her that day" | "Let's visit her." | No | No participant expressed actual intent to visit in the chat.
 |

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. Contextual Fit
Addresses subject, intent, and theme appropriately
.
Callouts: Subjective. If relationship/context is unclear, favor conventionally appropriate (formal/serious) replies over those requiring familiarity
.
Examples: | Input | Suggested Reply | Grade | Rationale | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | "just got promoted!" | "That's awesome! Congrats!" | Fit | Suitable celebration.
 | | "grandmother passed away" | "Did you see the game?" | Misfit | Fails to respect serious context.
 | | "stressed about deadline" | "Deadlines aren't real lol" | Misfit | Edge case; favor less playful reply when relationship is unknown.
 | | "help with presentation?" | "Sure! I know you're presenting to the board tomorrow" | Fit | Suitable, though penalized under Groundedness for the "board" detail.
 |

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. Conciseness
Complete and clear without redundancy
.
Examples: | Input | Suggested Reply | Grade | Rationale | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | "Lunch at 11?" | "Sounds good!" | Concise | Clear and complete.
 | | "Museum itinerary?" | "Well, I think that maybe we could possibly consider..." | Not Concise | Wordy and unclear.
 | | "Long time no see! meet up?" | "Ok" | Not Concise | Too brief; lacks completeness.
 |

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. Tone & Empathy Alignment
Reflects emotional quality via word choice, punctuation, formatting, emojis, and structure
.
Callouts: Consider Relationship/Formality labels. If in doubt, favor empathy
.
Examples: | Input | Suggested Reply | Grade | Rationale | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | "nervous about interview" | "You got this! crush it" | Aligned | Encouraging.
 | | "partner broke up with me" | "Breakups happen. Move on" | Not aligned | Cold and dismissive.
 | | "failed driving test" | "Aw, you'll get it next time" | Not aligned | Can be seen as low-effort/dismissive; favor acknowledging frustration.
 |

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. Naturalness
Sounds like authentic human conversation
.
Examples: | Input | Suggested Reply | Grade | Rationale | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | "See, my brother looks like Tom Cruise" | "Haha no way, that's wild" | Natural | Conversational phrasing.
 | | "worried these days" | "I acknowledge your aforementioned concern" | Unnatural | Overly formal for messaging.
 | | "shared the doc" | "PROCESSING. STANDBY FOR RESPONSE." | Unnatural | Looks machine-generated.
 |

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. Localization
Adapts to local language, culture, and formats
.
Failures include:
Awkward machine-translation.
Incorrect mixed languages (unless expected like Hinglish).
Wrong date/unit/punctuation formats (e.g., MM/DD vs DD/MM).
Scrambled symbols
.
Scenarios NOT considered issues:
English proper names in other languages.
Base language with small untranslated portion (e.g., "Send 你好嗎 to Ching Liu")
.

Step 5 - Personalization
Measures how well the reply captures the user's unique communication style (User Profile)
.
User Profile Components
Component
Description
Common phrases
Frequently used messages/emojis.
Abbreviations & Contractions
Preference for "cant" vs "cannot". Use of "can't" or "let's" is still considered personalized.
Punctuations
Preferred style and frequency.
Nickname
Names used for participants.
Sample Profile Patterns:
Capitalization: Rare (Sentence-Start).
Exclamation Points: Frequent (Excitement).
Ellipses: Frequent (Trailing/Pausing)
.
Contractions: Prefers full words (Rare "cant")
.
Grading Scales
Personalized: Clearly reflects style, tone, and patterns.
Contextually Adapted: Deviates from style but adapts appropriately to context/formality.
Generic: Lacks unique style; a personalized reply would have been appropriate.
Mismatched: Directly contradicts style (e.g., wrong tone, punctuation, or all caps)
.
Grading Principles:
Guidelines are a framework, not strict mandates.
User profile shows tendencies, not absolute rules.
Assess the likelihood the reply aligns with the user's "feel"
.
Examples: | Input Context | Suggested Reply | Grade | Rationale | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | "brother looks like Tom Cruise" | "Haha no way, that's wild!" | Personalized | Reflects informal style/exclamations.
 | | "nervous about interview" | "I understand that interviews can be daunting..." | Mismatched | Overly formal compared to profile.
 | | "free tonight?" | "Cant tonight, free tomorrow?" | Personalized | Matches profile preference for "cant".
 | | "free tonight?" | "YES! COUNT ME IN!" | Mismatched | Profile doesn't suggest all-caps tendency.
 |

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 6 - Comparison
Compare Assistant A vs. Assistant B as complete groups
.
Group Assistant A's suggestions (A1, A2) and Assistant B's (B1, B2).
Decide which group is more helpful/useful overall.
Example: If Assistant A has one suggestion and Assistant B has two, compare $ A1 $ vs the combined group of $ (B1 + B2) $
.
